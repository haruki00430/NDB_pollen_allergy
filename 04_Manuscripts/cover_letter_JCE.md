Dear Editor-in-Chief,

I am writing on behalf of all co-authors to submit our manuscript entitled **"How Outcome Definition Changes Ecological Inference in Prescription-Based Epidemiology: A Natural Experiment"** for consideration as an **Original Research Article** in the *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*.

**Scope and relevance to JCE**

This manuscript addresses a core question in clinical epidemiology methods: how does the choice of a medication-based outcome instrument influence ecological inference when individual diagnosis-linked records are unavailable? We use Japan's 47 prefectures and seasonal pollen exposure as a tractable natural experiment — not as an etiological claim about pollen and allergy per se, but as a case study with a well-characterised biological mechanism that allows the outcome's measurement properties to be empirically evaluated.

The study's contribution is methodological: we demonstrate that pharmacological specificity of the outcome, held constant across identical exposure data, covariates, and regression frameworks, explains a 1.72-fold difference in recoverable ecological signal (R² = 0.469 versus 0.272). A pharmacologically unrelated negative control confirmed that these differences reflected outcome specificity rather than ecological confounding. These findings directly inform the design of prescription-based ecological studies wherever diagnosis linkage is unavailable — including routine health data settings in any country. We believe this work aligns with JCE's focus on developing and evaluating outcome measures, assessing measurement validity, and improving study design methods for observational research.

**Addressing potential scope concerns**

We wish to proactively address two scope considerations noted in JCE's Guide for Authors:

1. *Pharmacovigilance and pharmacoepidemiology studies*: This manuscript is not a pharmacovigilance or pharmacosurveillance study, nor does it estimate causal drug effects. Rather, it treats prescription dispensing records as measurement instruments and evaluates their validity properties — a question of outcome measurement methodology applicable to any aggregate health data setting.

2. *Generalisability beyond a single country*: Although our data derive from Japan's NDB Open Data, the methodological principle — that pharmacological specificity shapes ecological signal recovery — is directly applicable to any jurisdiction using aggregate prescription or claims data (e.g., NHS Digital, Medicare, SNDS). The study's value lies in the analytical framework, not in Japan-specific findings.
